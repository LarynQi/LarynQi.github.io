Ana Fi Intizarak

by Umm Kulthum

  • Traditional takht ensemble with Umm Kulthum's powerful, grainy voice driving the song
  • Live sound with audience back-and-forth interaction with Umm Kulthum
  • Stays true to tarab by being nearly completely homophonic
    • Strings and Umm Kulthum share the same melody for the majority of the piece
  • Date: 1943

Khayef Aqul Yalli Fi Qalbi

by Abd al-Wahhab

  • Features traditional tarab instruments, but they are overshadowed by the introduction of the unfamiliar electronic syntehsizer
  • Contrasting melodies between Abd al-Wahhab and the violins as opposed to the homophonic nature of traditional songs
  • Lacks a prominent traditional Arabic rhythm/beat (Iqa')
  • Absence of any prominent audience goes to show how moernists musicians at the time were embracing recording technologies and moving toward more meticulously produced songs
  • Compared to the songs from like Ana Fi Intizarak, this track is very short, reflecting the physical limitations of records
  • Date: N/A

Enta Omri

by Umm Kulthum and Abd al-Wahhab

  • Steady percussion backbone
  • Features instrumental breaks (e.g. starting at 53:36)
  • Dual emphasis on Umm Kulthum's powerful voice and the unique, modern/western instruments (especially the guitar at 4:05) which are characteristic of Abd al-Wahhab’s music as hinted at by the long instrumental intro
  • Date: 1964

Ahwak

by Abd al-Halim

  • Features a very vivid and emotional intro
    • Once Halim comes in around 0:33, the song is almost entirely driven by him until an instrumental break starting at 1:52
  • Rich, mellow, and sentimental crooning technique performed by Abd al-Halim (made possible by new microphone technologies
  • Although Abd al-Halim’s singing is very smooth and gentle, his sound is still able to “float” above the orchestration, making it seem like he is singing right in front of you while the the instruments play in the background behind him (thanks to the more sensitive, directional microphones)
  • Date: 1957

Awwal Marra

by Abd al-Halim

  • Intro features use of piano (not traditional to tarab ensemble)
  • The use of whistling showcases interesting some of the applications of microphone technology
  • Contains elements of traditional tarab music such as strings echoing the main vocal line, call-and-response with strings, and use of tambourine percussion
  • Date: 1957

Jana al-Hawa

by Abd al-Halim

  • Despite being in front of a live and presumably large audience, Abd al-Halim’s voice remains tender in contrast to Umm Kulthum’s more aggressive, grainy, and driving voice
  • Halim engages in participatory singing with his audience during certain segments (3:16)
  • Percussion features popular and traditional rhythmic mode called Sa'idi(can be heard prominently at 5:40)
  • 5:50 features an accordion solo
  • Date: 1969

Zahma

by Ahmed Adaweya

  • Very fast paced
  • Call-and-response between the sentimentalist violin and singer make it feel very driven and passionate
  • Traditional in its use of violin, ney, and traditional percussion instruments, repetitive structure, and segments of background vocalists leading the song, implying a more participatory setting
  • Artist background
    • Didn’t consider anything political or otherwise when writing his music
  • Date: 1980

El Salamou Aleikom

by Hakim

  • Laid back with background vocalists casually repeating the main lyrical phrases
  • Distinct electronic feel, specifically through the trap set percussion, autotune, and sampled vocal parts
  • Branches off from traditional folk music with its divergence from tarab instruments toward western pop instruments such as accordion and brass instruments
  • Artist background
    • Wrote many of his songs for the purpose of being digestible for huge crowds of people (e.g. mahraganat)
    • Came from a family with social standing
  • Date: 2002

Tarab

Traditional Arabic Music

  • Arabic music was codified in Egypt in the early 20th century
  • This traditional Arabic music featured 4 key elements
    • Maqam (melodic modes)
    • Iqa' (rhythmic modes)
    • Specific form and folk-inspired genre known as tarab
      • Homophonic (featured only one melody and no harmony)
      • A lot of improvisation and call-and-response style of music
      • Centered around a constant feedback loop between performers and audience
    • Particular instruments including Hanun, Rahood, Ney, and Violin
  • Date: Early 20th Century

New Technologies

Microphone, Phonograph, Radio, Cinema

  • Diminished the live aspect of Arabic music and the feedback loop
  • Added a physical constraint to the length of tracks (3-3.5 minute songs on records)
  • As the tech was first introduced, it had limitations
    • Microphones couldn't pick up very quiet or loud playing (volume range was minimized)
  • Created more roles in creating music
    • Composer, lyricist, producer, sound engineer, performer, etc.
  • Changes in culture occurred due to new tech
    • High culture was defined by those who could engage with expensive music (cafe culture)
  • New mediums allowed for Egyptian music to spread throughout the region (and even into Europe)
  • Date: 1920s

The Rise of Nasser

Polticial Changes

  • The term "Naserism" is used to describe the poltiical climate of Egypt under Nasser's rule
  • Nationalism:
    • Staunch advocate of Arab unity
    • Supported Arab resistance to the state of Israel
    • Stood for unification of Arabs in support of Palestinians
  • Pan-Arabism ideology:
    • Politicized "Arab-ness"
  • Public radio and film was owned by the government during these times which opened the gates for politics to influence music
  • Date: 1956

The Golden Age

Flourishing Culture and Arts

  • Drastic innovation and variation in the general realm of the arts, including radio, film, and music
  • In great part due to the gradual acceptance of new, industry-defining technologies
  • What sparked changes in what defined popular music in Egypt
  • Shift away from high-class songs to more light-hearted subjects
  • Many popular Golden Age artists, such as Umm Kulthum and Abd al-Wahhab doubled-up in their careers as both musicians and film stars and musicals gained popularity
  • Date: Mid-20th Century

Six-day War

Egypt, Jordan, and Syria against Israel

  • Popular singer Umm Kulthum used her public radio broadcasts to promote the war efforts and ask for donations, politicizing her career
  • Egypt aligned under a country-wide sentiment of nationalism and populism
  • Egypt was crushed and lost within 6 days
  • Despite losing, Umm Kulthum continued to hold political benefit concerts to spread pan-Arab sentiments and culture
  • Date: 1967

Sha'bi

The Music of the People

  • Drew on Egyptian working class/folk sentiments
  • Sha'bi artists such as Ahmed Adaweya were able to capitalize on the new cassette technologies on the 1970s to circulate new and refreshing music (Cassette Revolution)
  • Artists focused their music on expressing everyday and uninteresting desires and situations
  • Instrumentation included ney, tambourine, strings, and other tarab insturments although played in a different style and with a different purpose
    • Still contained elements of call-and-response
  • Often featured fast paced songs and belly-dancers
  • Date: 1970s-1980s

Electro Sha'bi

New Age Working Class Music

  • Focused on accepting hardship with resilience and happiness in the face of it
  • Really tried to speak to the working class
  • Clear move away from tarab instruments and style
    • Included pop instruments such as bass guitar, trap set, and synthesizer as well as brass instruments and auto-tuning
    • Call-and-response and audience was still present, but not as prominent
    • Meant to be consumed for huge crowds of people unlike tarab
    • Included Western harmonies and chords
    • Song structure and form more closely reflected Western pop songs with a distinct verse, chorus, and bridge
  • Mahraganats or festivals occurred on the streets of poor neighborhoods and united people
    • Defined by loudness and tension that reflected anger surrounding class disparities
  • Date: Turn of the 21st Century

Liner Notes

Playlist

In this playlist, you will listen to key artists from the Golden Age (mid 20th century) of music in Egypt including Umm Kulthum, Abd al-Wahhab, and Abd al-Halim. Even among just these three artists, you will hear distinct styles that reflect the changing political and technological atmosphere of Egypt during these times. Note in particular the traditionalist and singer-driven style of Umm Kulthum, the showcasing of electronic sounds and foreign beats of Abd al-Wahhab’s music, and the sentimental crooning of Abd al-Halim’s tracks. In order to further contextualize these artists, this playlist also features tracks from before the Golden Age (early 20th century) and post-Golden Age (turn of 21st century). From these tracks, you’ll gain a better understanding of the traditional takht sound that artists like Umm Kulthum sought to uphold as well as how Egyptian music began to incorporate more and more foreign musical elements and continued to innovate.
  • Date: 19xx-19xx
